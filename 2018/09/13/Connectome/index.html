Connectome: From Structure to Function

Posted on 2018-09-13 |
Words count in article: 5.2k | Reading time ≈ 32

This essay was for the midterm examination of curriculum Introduction to Neuroscience when I was in my junior year of university. It took me about one month to read over 50 papers, and two weeks to draft and to complete. It is still easy to read and understand, in my opinion, and scientific enough to give you the overview of the connectome. Hope you all enjoy it.

From Human Connectome Project

Abstract

Our brain is a complicated network which is unified for structural and functional system. However, currently our knowledge is fairly scarce, while it is essential to learn our brain for the progress of humanity. This article will introduce the concept, meanwhile also an undergoing project, called the connectome. The background and history of connectome are set out first, from the time when Cajal opened the new door of brain science, to one decade ago when this concept was put forward to the public. Then three scales are briefly introduced which is divided to do the research more efficiently, on the basis of the structure and imaging resolution. In addition, the techniques and approaches utilized and the tendency they may development towards are illustrated as well. Finally, a general conclusion on connectome is made, including the current applications and bright prospects which is to be seen recently and in the future.

Introduction

Networks are everywhere nowadays. When we pick up a fallen leaf, we can see the veins on it, which is the network under the leaf surface. When we go out of our house, facing toward our destination, there is a big network consisting of roads and intersections in front of us. And of course, the Internet, which we are both familiar and strange with. For most of us, we use it every day, without knowing what the certain principles is inside. Therefore, we may get kind of helpless, even a little bit desperate, when something is wrong with the Internet, with nobody owing professional knowledge around. For human being, Internet, once created by our own hand, is so complicated that we struggled to learn to learn it. Let alone those networks that were masterpieces created by the nature and is still largely unknown to us.

In our brain, there exists a mysterious network. As we all know, the most functional unit of our brain is one type of cell, called neurons. There are millions of neurons in human’s brain, making it obvious that some certain interconnections and interactions must exist between the massive individuals. One neuron is composed of soma, axons and dendrites. The information transports from one neuron to another through synapses, which are formed by axons from one neuron and dendrites from another. Several neurons collaborate, to function as a circuit. Several circuits collaborate, to function as a sub-area of the brain. Several sub-areas collaborate, to make our brains work.

The term “Connectome” was first seen in 2005, suggested by Dr. Olaf Sporns at Indiana University and Dr. Patric Hagmann at Lausanne University Hospital. The connectome is a comprehensive map of neural connections in the brain. As Dr. Olaf Sporns wrote in his paper, “The connectome will significantly increase our understanding of how functional brain states emerge from their underlying structural substrate and will provide new mechanistic insights into how brain function is affected if this structural substrate is disrupted. It will provide a unified, time-invariant, and readily available neuroinformatic resources that could be used in virtually all areas of experimental and theoretical neuroscience.’’1

In fact, the term is inspired by genome, another ongoing project with periodic fruit. The two subjects are indeed comparable. Dr. Patric Hagmann pointed out the similarities between genome and connectome in his paper, “It is clear that, like the genome, which is much more than just a juxtaposition of genes, the set of all neuronal connections in the brain is much more than the sum of their individual components. The genome is an entity itself, as it is from the subtle gene interaction that life emerges. In a similar manner, one could consider the brain connectome, set of all neuronal connections, as one single entity, thus emphasizing the fact that the huge brain neuronal communication capacity and computational power critically relies on this subtle and incredibly complex connectivity architecture.”2

As is mentioned above, there are quite many similarities between connectome and genome. At first, they are both fundamental structural data sets with regard to the unit they function. Also, there are many levels in the two concepts, especially according to different scales, with synapse, neurons, circuits and sub-region in connectome, while with nucleotide, histones, genes, and chromosome in genome. Thirdly, both of them are different across different species, or different individuals of the same species, or the same species from different period in the long river of time. In addition, although in one individual, they are variable, probably due to environment, requirements for different functions or the spatial proximity.3-5 At last, complex system dynamics are produced, for instance, the firing patterns of neurons and the spatiotemporal expression of genes.6

Just like the time when the genome project first came up, there are many objections and worries against the connectome project, such as insight limitation, poor using of scarce source, seeming impossible task and so on.7 Anyway so far there have been some achievements towards the goal. About 30 years ago, a work which was consistent with the connectome virtually have been accomplished. The structure of the nervous system of C. elegans was fully constructed.8-10 There are exactly 302 neurons, without varying structures in C. elegans, making it convenient for study. Although the structure of the nervous system of nematode is much simpler than other species, especially the mammals, the complete data set is still too huge to be handled. Furthermore, partial connectomes of a mouse retina and that of a mouse primary visual cortex have been reconstructed.11,12 What’s more, in 2014, The Allen Mouse Brain Connectivity Atlas presented a mesoscale connectivity of the adult mouse brain, which is available as an open-access public database.13

The ultimate goal is to reconstruct human connectome, which is, “a comprehensive structural description of the network of elements and connections forming the human brain.”1,2 There is no doubt that the project is significant for us human being. Only if we know what the structure of our brain is like, can we say it is possible to learn the unambiguous function. Otherwise, everything we are doing is just like blind monks touching the elephant to try to depict it, without any directions or strategies or efficiency. There are many groups devoting themselves to explore the secret of human connectome, while two of them in the lead, both of which were awarded Human Connectome Project (HCP) granted by NIH in 2010. One is led by Washington University, the University of Minnesota, and Oxford University, called the “Wu-Minn” HCP consortium, and the other is led by Massachusetts General Hospital and University of California at Los Angeles, called the MGH-UCLA HCP consortium.14

Scales and Levels

As is similar to genome, which can be studied in different scales of base pairs, nucleotides, genes, chromosomes and so on, there are different scales to watch the connectome. Generally speaking, these scales can be categorized as microscale, mesoscale, and macroscale, intelligible as the name suggests, defined according to different levels of spatial resolution in brain imaging. Different scales provide different fundamental units and elements to study, different methods to utilize, different challenges to face, and different results to get in the end. As a result of that it is quite significant for us to learn about what the certain scales are, and what information they can provide.

Microscale

It is the most exquisite scale among the three mentioned above, which means the units and elements we are facing is the most fundamental. It is a world between neurons and synapses, just like what we see under the electron microscope. In microscale, we need to know every interaction between every neuron. It is not as simple as one-to-one correspondence. As we all know, the axon of one neuron forms synapses with the spines of the dendrites on another neuron. The problem is, there are many spines on one dendrite, and there are many dendrites on one neuron. Besides, there can be multiple spots which is capable for forming synapse on one axon terminate, as is suggested in rodent mesoaccumbens axons.15

The consequence is that there are about 1010 neurons in human cerebral cortex, and 1014 synaptic connections between them.16 For now, there is no efficient techniques and to do the mass data collection, and we also have no reliable method or theory to do the data analyzing. In addition, the synaptic structures are variable though different times,3,17 which lifts the dimensions of the problem itself and the difficulties for multiple levels. It seems that the variable remodeling of spines may have an influence on the synaptic connections of abundant potential synaptic sites.1,18,19 In conclusion, apart from the difficulties we may come across while studying connectome in the microscale, the necessity and effectiveness are also worth questioning.

Macroscale

According to the normal logic, the order in which I introduce the three scales is from the smallest to the biggest, or the reverse. However, once upon I finished the microscale part, preparing for the mesoscale part in details, I found the concept of mesoscale is ambiguous, while that of macroscale is much clearer. It is like the two sides are almost decided, so there is much space for the middle to adjust itself. As a result, I decided to change my order, putting the macroscale part in front of the mesoscale part.

Although the neurons have their own significance in the whole system, some of them have very common points that make them work collaboratively, implementing different functions. If we recognize these similar neurons as a whole part, the brain can be briefly divided into several regions, with their own significance of existing and function. The earliest classification of brain regions is developed by Franz Joseph in the nineteenth century. He came up with a discipline called phrenology, divided the brain into several regions marking different mental functions, like ideality, hope and consciousness.20 As our knowledge about brain gets deeper, the division of brain regions have been modified for many times. The basis of the division of the brain region is that all the “structural elements share highly similar long-range connectivity patterns”, and that “these patterns are dissimilar between brain regions”.1 Anyway, no matter how the regions change, they are the nodes that make up the brain graph in macroscale.

Mesoscale

As is mentioned above, the definition of mesoscale is quite ambiguous, between those of microscale and macroscale. However, many scientists prefer the word “neuron population” to define the tricky concept.1,6,13,21 It is under a spatial resolution of hundreds of micrometers, containing hundreds or thousands of individual neurons. According to Dr. Olaf Sporns, the definition of cortical minicolumn is an appropriate example.1 The dimeter of one minicolumn is about 30-50 micrometers, containing approximately 80-100 individual neurons.22 As the same as the rule of macroscale, it is also required to be highly similar with the others in one area, and to show difference compared to external properties.

It is no doubt that the mesoscale and macroscale view are the best two for connectome studying. And in fact, there has been some effectual practice in these two scales. A mesoscale connectome of the mouse brain has been reconstructed by Allen Institute for Brain Science,13 and was further later.23 The macroscale connectivity are also be visualized in mouse24 and human brain.25 It is believed that the combination of data of connectivity in micro-, meso-, and macroscale will be complementary to each other, making it more convenient, comprehensive, and deep for us to understand the connectome.

Techs and Methods

In the recent one century, scientists who are studying the connectivity of neurons in the brain have used three main methods, the single-cell impregnation, the optically based tract-tracing and electron microscopy.26 These methods have helped us solve many problems and make the great discoveries, yet, may have more and more drawbacks and restrictions as the technology and our work progresses. In this part, I will introduce some current methods, including but not limited to those mentioned above, and some ideal ones.

The single-cell staining by dye impregnation is the very first approach to study the brain. It can be traced back to the nineteenth century, when Cajal began to watch the nervous system using Golgi staining. It is known what happened next, and where are standing is based on the great discovery and the great approach. Nevertheless, in spite of its contribution, staining, whether the past or current method, has still not solved some problems. Firstly, because of the light diffraction, there cannot be a high resolution, which makes it impossible to discern in a subtle degree. Secondly, the distance that the chemicals can travel is limited, making it too hard to see the overall situation to make an accurate judgement.

To make the long-distance observation feasible, scientists then used tract-tracing techniques, in which surgeries were usually required. Afterwards, it was replaced by techniques in which labels was put in certain areas and then diffused to the target regions. There is a wide range of labels, such as radioactive amino acids or sugars, proteins, enzymes and fluorescent organic molecules.27 As the genetic approaches developments, labels like fluorescent proteins can be encoded in certain times or regions to express, under the control of regulatory elements or viral vectors.26,28-32 However, a coin has its two sides. Tract-tracing techniques can only trace large neuron populations and single neural pathways, which makes it not so useful if not combining with other complementary approaches.

For another, electron microscopy can resolve the resolution problem, for it is the most observing techniques nowadays. Electron microscopy played an important role in the construction of connectome of C. elegans in 1986.8,33 For now, there are many modified combinational methods on the basis of observing via the electron microscopy. Just like 3D print, we can put the tissue into very thin slices, watching and analyzing the information on each individual slice, finally reconstructing them. A new technique utilizing a tape-like structure, makes the cutting and collecting consecutive and automated.34 Nevertheless, more delicate, more work. Using the slices, which is full of detailed information, means unimaginable information to collect, analyze and integrate. At least currently, it seems lack of hope to do the huge work with techniques we master, let alone by hand.

In addition, there is also a problem that the construction of the nerve system requires explicit identification of each neurons we are interested. The process cannot be done if there is only one or two single labels being used. Brainbow is such an approach that aiming at this problem. Just as its name, which is inspired by rainbow, a beautiful seven-color natural phenomenon, the function if brainbow is to label individual neurons in stochastic colors, via the expression of multiple fluorescent proteins.35 The Human Connectome Project even makes a gallery to exhibit the gorgeous artwork-like pictures which shows the structure of the white matter in colors. However, as many other approaches, brainbow has its own limitations. For instance, the diffraction-limited resolution is still a problem. Also, the effect of color display needs to be improved further, such as the brightness, distinguishing degree. What’s more, the expression in projection neurons are high, while that in interneurons are poor.36

Inspired by brainbow, here came a novel approach called BOINC, which stands for barcoding of individual neuronal connections.37 This approach utilizes the convenience and speediness of DNA sequencing. Each neuron is labeled by a unique sequence of nucleotides, just like a barcode. And these barcodes from neurons which are synaptically connected are associated together. As a result, the two types of barcodes, representing one neuron in a sense, is paired, and then be sequenced of high throughput. It is considered to be potential to be cheap and fast.

It seems there is a lot of work that has been and is being done. However, those works are on the basis of static state. Imaging is not a gif-format animation, or a movie. It can only capture a stop motion for a very short-time moment. For another reason, currently most of the imaging works are conducted post mortem, which means we cannot adjust our experiments for further study, on the same individual, much less real-time monitoring. Some non-invasive imaging techniques provide new approaches about solving the problem.

MRI, the abbreviation of magnetic resonance imaging, has been a traditional and common approach for imaging the modality of brain.38 It doesn’t use ionizing radiation, and the data it acquired can be applied for both clinical and research subjects. Repeated scanning of the same individual is also viable. However, the drawback is a long-time stationary state is required, which is difficult for animals. Anyway, MRI provides a basic tech platform that many other techniques are born from it, like dMRI, standing for diffusion MRI, and fMRI, standing for functional MRI.39 The first provides cubic-millimeter-resolution construction of white-matter tracts,25 while the second makes it feasible to capture the activity of the brain, revealing a universal functional architecture.40

As we acquire structural and functional data, it is time to combine them with some appropriate logic and methods. Four steps are needed to achieve this goal.41 Firstly, it is essential to determine the parcellation of the brain, and then obtain the data for both structural and functional aiming approaches. Secondly, express the structural linkage and functional coupling. Thirdly, build a connection matrix aggregating both the structural and functional network. Lastly, network analysis can be started by computing and extracting relevant information from the two matrix.6

It is obviously not enough to achieve human connectome. The follow-up work was suggested by Dr. Olaf Sporns in his milestone-like paper in 2005.1 After obtaining the data from network analysis, it is required to test and verify the human connectome. We can perform population analysis of healthy subjects and spatial registration to adjust the result acquired by all of computing to the reality. In addition, we can compare connectome of healthy human with that of patients or compare connectome of human being with that of other species evolutionally next to us, like the macaque. There is inestimable information and value waiting to be discovered.

Challenges

Complexity

As is through the whole article, the reason we spent decades and decades, centuries and centuries, on observing, depicting, dividing and analyzing the brain, and the reason we have and still being developed techniques and approaches one after another, and the reason we are making new discoveries, overthrowing and pruning the previous view, is not only the brain is so important to us, but also it is so complex and variable. The complexity has been involved above but is far more than that. For any levels in the system, ranging from neurons to brain region, is much more complicated than we could imagine. For instance, we are devoting to finding the connections between the structure and function. However, there is a large number of functional connections between regions that are not directedly connect, in anatomical view.42 The complexity increases the difficulty for us to observe the structure, dispose the data, look for the latent connections, select the appropriate approach, learn and understand the function.

Individual Variability

Just like the genome, connectome represents a relatively common characteristic of one specie. However, as to individuals, they can have their own connectome. As we all know, everybody of us are different from others’, for we have our own DNA, which carries our own genetic material. The DNA determines what our connectome is like, so our own connectome is different from others’. Apart from genetic differences, there are many other causes resulting in individual connectome variable, such as developmental and experiential history, gender difference, pathologies and responses to injury.1 If connectome is anticipated to make contributions to certain areas, such as diseases, the individual variability cannot be neglected.

Parcellations and Scales

Another challenge is the criterion of parcellations and scales of the brain. Although there are three recognized relatively clear scales, a certain level of resolution to complete the connectome is still under the debate. With current techniques, we may begin the project from mesoscale and macroscale. It can be foreseen that as we learn more about the nerve system, especially the tinier functional structure than synapses, or even, transmitters, and as we invent more powerful machines and tools, there seems to be an endless processing. And the parcellation also matters, for whether the accurate boundaries of different regions can be found. The lack of knowledge to our brain is the restrictions to achieve the goal, which needs more work along with the whole brain science.

Techs

In essence, the lack of knowledge is due to the undeveloped technology. Currently, the acquisition, storage and manipulation of raw data is still a headache problem. It is estimated that a single microscale human connectome may take up one trillion gigabytes.43 There are also a large number of choke points on our way leading to the truth. As to connectome, there are majorly two types of techniques that are badly required. One is imaging, which can make it clear at a very high resolution, identifying different components at the same time, and the other is analyzing, making it automated and highly efficient to processing abundant data set. Looking back on the history of neuroscience, or a bit wider, the bioscience, each breakthrough made to the field and for human, always came after a new revolutionary technique. The only way to make a breakthrough is the invention of new techniques. Particularly outstanding discoveries and outcomes cannot be made if the technology doesn’t make any progress.

Outcome

The direction from basic research for brain science to connectome is no doubt correct. Brain plays such an important role that if the relationship between its structure and function is figured out, imagine what great changes will be brought to our lives! Our intelligence may improve, and we may even amend the personalities that we do not want. We can even get to know what happens in our brain when we are retrieving our certain memory. If you have seen the famous science fiction TV serious Black Mirror, you may not be strange to the scene that the roles in one episode can reviewing and deleting their memory, as seems fantastic but possibly come true someday in the future. Moreover, some serious diseases, like the Parkinson’s disease and the tristimania, may be cured. Several progresses have been made through recent years such as the disorder connectivities of brain in Schizophrenia,44 and aging.45 It is also possible for disabled people who have lost their fingers or hands to regain the ability to seize tiny things. The potential of connectome is difficult to estimate and imagine, and so is the profit that can be brought to us. What we are waiting for is a revolutionary researching system, or just an approach, to help us integrating and analyzing the massive raw data. The day when comes the revolution, is the day when we decipher our brain, and is the day when we can change the world.
